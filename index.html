Origami is the art of paper folding.
Its name derives from Japanese words ori (“folding”) and kami (“paper”).
Traditional origami consists of folding a single sheet of square paper (often with a colored side) into a sculpture without cutting, gluing, taping, or even marking it.

Origami History
Paper was invented in China by Cai Lun. Papermaking skills subsequently migrated to Japan by 610. In 1680 a short poem by poet Ihara Saikaku references butterfly origami, revealing how well engrained in Japanese culture paper folding had become by that time. One of the earliest known paper-folding instruction books was Akisato Rito’s Sembazuru orikata (1797), and it showed how to fold linked cranes cut and folded from a square of paper.
